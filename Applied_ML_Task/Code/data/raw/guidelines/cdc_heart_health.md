# CDC Heart Health Recommendations

Heart disease is the leading cause of death in the United States, killing about 697,000 Americans every year — that is 1 in every 5 deaths. The term "heart disease" refers to several types of heart conditions, the most common being coronary artery disease (CAD), which affects blood flow to the heart.

## Key Risk Factors

Several health conditions, lifestyle choices, and age/family history can increase risk for heart disease:

- High blood pressure: Uncontrolled high blood pressure raises risks for heart attack and stroke. It's called the "silent killer" because it usually has no symptoms.
- High cholesterol: High levels of LDL cholesterol can lead to plaque buildup in arteries. Total cholesterol levels above 200 mg/dL increase heart disease risk.
- Diabetes: Adults with diabetes are nearly twice as likely to have heart disease or stroke compared to those without diabetes. High blood sugar damages blood vessels over time.
- Smoking: Cigarette smoking raises blood pressure and increases risk for heart attack and stroke. The chemicals in tobacco smoke damage the heart and blood vessels.
- Obesity: Excess weight increases strain on the heart and is linked to high blood pressure, high cholesterol, and diabetes.
- Physical inactivity: Not getting enough physical activity raises the risk of heart disease. Regular exercise strengthens the heart muscle and helps control weight, cholesterol, and blood pressure.
- Excessive alcohol use: Drinking too much alcohol can raise blood pressure and increase risk of heart disease. It can also increase levels of triglycerides, a type of fat in the blood.
- Unhealthy diet: Diets high in saturated fats, trans fats, cholesterol, sodium, and added sugars can increase heart disease risk.

## Prevention Strategies

CDC recommends the following evidence-based strategies:

1. Get regular physical activity: At least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity such as brisk walking, or 75 minutes per week of vigorous-intensity aerobic activity such as running. Include muscle-strengthening activities at least 2 days per week.

2. Eat a healthy diet: Focus on fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean meats, fish, beans, and fat-free or low-fat dairy products. Limit saturated fats, trans fats, added sugars, and sodium (less than 2,300 mg per day, ideally less than 1,500 mg for most adults).

3. Maintain a healthy weight: Body Mass Index (BMI) between 18.5 and 24.9 is considered healthy. Even losing 5-10% of body weight can significantly reduce heart disease risk factors.

4. Manage stress: Chronic stress may contribute to heart disease, especially if it leads to unhealthy coping mechanisms like smoking, overeating, or drinking. Practice stress management through physical activity, meditation, or counseling.

5. Get enough sleep: Adults should aim for 7-9 hours of sleep per night. Poor sleep quality and short sleep duration are associated with increased risk for high blood pressure, obesity, and diabetes.

6. Control blood pressure: Know your numbers and work with your healthcare provider to keep blood pressure below 120/80 mmHg. Take prescribed medications as directed.

7. Manage cholesterol: Get cholesterol checked every 4-6 years if you are at normal risk, more frequently if you have elevated risk. Follow treatment plans including diet, exercise, and medication if prescribed.

8. Manage diabetes: If you have diabetes, monitor your blood sugar regularly and follow your treatment plan. Keep your A1C level below 7% as recommended by your healthcare provider.

9. Don't smoke: If you smoke, quit. Avoid secondhand smoke. Within 1 year of quitting, your heart disease risk drops by half.

## Warning Signs

Heart attack warning signs include chest pain or discomfort, upper body pain or discomfort in the arms, back, neck, jaw, or upper stomach, shortness of breath, nausea, lightheadedness, or cold sweats. Call 911 immediately if you or someone else experiences these symptoms.

Stroke warning signs follow the F.A.S.T. method: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call 911.

## Screening Recommendations

Adults should have blood pressure checked at least once every 2 years if readings are normal (below 120/80 mmHg). Cholesterol should be checked every 4-6 years for adults at normal risk. Blood glucose testing should begin at age 45 or earlier if overweight with additional risk factors.
