# NICE Guidelines on Hypertension in Adults (NG136)

## Diagnosis and Assessment

Hypertension should be diagnosed using ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM) or home blood pressure monitoring (HBPM) to confirm the diagnosis after a clinic reading of 140/90 mmHg or higher. Stage 1 hypertension is defined as clinic blood pressure of 140/90 mmHg or higher and subsequent ABPM daytime average or HBPM average of 135/85 mmHg or higher. Stage 2 hypertension is clinic blood pressure of 160/100 mmHg or higher and subsequent ABPM daytime average or HBPM average of 150/95 mmHg or higher. Severe hypertension is clinic systolic blood pressure of 180 mmHg or higher, or clinic diastolic blood pressure of 120 mmHg or higher.

## Lifestyle Interventions

NICE recommends the following lifestyle interventions for all patients with hypertension, regardless of whether they are started on drug treatment:

- Diet: Adopt a healthy and varied diet including at least 5 portions of fruit and vegetables per day. Reduce total and saturated fat intake. Reduce salt intake to less than 6 g/day (roughly one teaspoon). Reduce caffeine consumption, as more than 4 cups of coffee per day may raise blood pressure.
- Exercise: Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise per week, such as cycling or fast walking, in bouts of 10 minutes or more. Encourage patients to incorporate exercise into daily routines, such as walking to work or using stairs instead of lifts.
- Alcohol: Advise patients to reduce alcohol intake. Men and women should not regularly drink more than 14 units per week, spread over 3 or more days.
- Weight: Maintain a healthy weight with a BMI of 20-25 kg/m2 and a waist circumference below 94 cm for men and below 80 cm for women.
- Smoking: Offer smoking cessation advice and support. While smoking does not directly cause sustained hypertension, it greatly increases the risk of cardiovascular disease.
- Relaxation therapies: Although routine provision of stress management or relaxation therapies is not recommended, patients should be informed that these may have a modest effect on blood pressure.

## Drug Treatment

### When to Start

- Stage 1 hypertension with target organ damage, cardiovascular disease, renal disease, diabetes, or a 10-year cardiovascular risk of 10% or more: Offer antihypertensive drug treatment.
- Stage 1 hypertension without the above: Consider treatment based on individual circumstances.
- Stage 2 hypertension: Offer antihypertensive drug treatment to all patients regardless of age.

### Treatment Steps

Step 1: Under 55 years or with type 2 diabetes — offer an ACE inhibitor or ARB. Aged 55 or over, or Black African/Caribbean origin — offer a calcium channel blocker.

Step 2: Combine ACE inhibitor or ARB with a calcium channel blocker. For Black African/Caribbean patients, consider an ARB rather than ACE inhibitor when combined with a calcium channel blocker.

Step 3: Combine ACE inhibitor or ARB with calcium channel blocker and thiazide-like diuretic.

Step 4 (Resistant hypertension): Consider adding spironolactone if blood potassium is 4.5 mmol/L or lower, or an alpha-blocker or beta-blocker if potassium is higher than 4.5 mmol/L.

## Blood Pressure Targets

For adults under 80 years: aim for clinic blood pressure below 140/90 mmHg and ABPM/HBPM below 135/85 mmHg. For adults aged 80 and over: aim for clinic blood pressure below 150/90 mmHg and ABPM/HBPM below 145/85 mmHg.

## Monitoring

Review patients with hypertension annually. Check blood pressure, provide lifestyle advice, and discuss medication adherence. Offer people with treated hypertension annual monitoring of renal function and electrolytes.
