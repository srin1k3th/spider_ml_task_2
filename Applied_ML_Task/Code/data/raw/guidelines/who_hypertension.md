# WHO Guidelines on Hypertension

Hypertension (high blood pressure) is when the pressure in your blood vessels is too high (systolic greater than or equal to 140 mmHg and/or diastolic greater than or equal to 90 mmHg). It is a common condition affecting around 1.28 billion adults aged 30-79 years worldwide, with most (two-thirds) living in low- and middle-income countries.

## Diagnosis

Blood pressure is recorded as two numbers: systolic (pressure when the heart beats) over diastolic (pressure when the heart rests between beats). Hypertension is diagnosed if, when measured on two different days, the systolic blood pressure readings on both days are greater than or equal to 140 mmHg and/or the diastolic blood pressure readings on both days are greater than or equal to 90 mmHg.

## Risk Factors

Modifiable risk factors include unhealthy diets (excessive salt consumption, a diet high in saturated fat and trans fats, low intake of fruits and vegetables), physical inactivity, consumption of tobacco and alcohol, and being overweight or obese. Non-modifiable risk factors include a family history of hypertension, age over 65 years, and co-existing diseases such as diabetes or kidney disease.

## Lifestyle Modifications

WHO recommends the following lifestyle changes to help manage and prevent hypertension:

- Reduce salt intake to less than 5 grams per day (slightly less than one teaspoon)
- Eat more fruits and vegetables, aiming for at least five servings per day
- Be physically active on a regular basis, at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity activity per week
- Avoid tobacco use in any form including cigarettes, cigars, pipes, or chewing tobacco
- Reduce and avoid harmful use of alcohol — WHO recommends no level of alcohol consumption is safe
- Limit intake of foods high in saturated fats and eliminate industrially produced trans fats
- Reduce and manage mental stress through relaxation techniques, adequate sleep, and social support

## Pharmacological Treatment

For patients who require medication, common first-line drug classes include ACE inhibitors, angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs), calcium channel blockers, and thiazide diuretics. Treatment should be individualized based on patient characteristics, comorbidities, and local drug availability. WHO recommends initiating treatment with a single pill combination (two drugs in one pill) to improve adherence.

## Complications

Uncontrolled hypertension can lead to serious health complications including heart attack, heart failure, stroke, kidney damage, vision loss, sexual dysfunction, and peripheral artery disease. It is the leading modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease and premature death globally.

## Monitoring

Patients with hypertension should have their blood pressure monitored regularly. Home blood pressure monitoring is recommended as it can help patients track their condition. Regular follow-up visits with healthcare providers are essential to assess treatment effectiveness and adjust medications if needed.
