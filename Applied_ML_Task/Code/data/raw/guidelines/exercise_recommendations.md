# Exercise and Physical Activity Recommendations

## Key Guidelines for Adults

Adults should move more and sit less throughout the day. Some physical activity is better than none. Adults who sit less and do any amount of moderate-to-vigorous physical activity gain some health benefits.

### Aerobic Activity

For substantial health benefits, adults should do at least 150 to 300 minutes a week of moderate-intensity aerobic physical activity, or 75 to 150 minutes a week of vigorous-intensity aerobic physical activity, or an equivalent combination of moderate- and vigorous-intensity aerobic activity. Additional health benefits are gained by engaging in physical activity beyond the equivalent of 300 minutes of moderate-intensity physical activity a week.

Examples of moderate-intensity activities: brisk walking (2.5 to 4 mph), recreational cycling (under 10 mph), water aerobics, dancing, gardening, doubles tennis, and active yoga.

Examples of vigorous-intensity activities: jogging or running, swimming laps, singles tennis, aerobic dancing, heavy gardening, hiking uphill, cycling over 10 mph, and jumping rope.

### Muscle-Strengthening Activity

Adults should do muscle-strengthening activities of moderate or greater intensity that involve all major muscle groups on 2 or more days a week. This includes activities like lifting weights, working with resistance bands, doing exercises that use body weight for resistance (push-ups, sit-ups), heavy gardening, and some forms of yoga.

### Balance Training

Older adults should incorporate multicomponent physical activity that includes balance training as well as aerobic and muscle-strengthening activities. Balance exercises include standing on one foot, heel-to-toe walking, tai chi, and balance board exercises.

## Benefits of Regular Physical Activity

Regular physical activity provides extensive health benefits:

- Cardiovascular health: Reduces risk of heart disease by 20-35% and stroke by 20-30%
- Blood pressure: Can lower systolic blood pressure by 5-8 mmHg in people with hypertension
- Cholesterol: Raises HDL (good) cholesterol and may reduce LDL (bad) cholesterol and triglycerides
- Blood sugar: Improves insulin sensitivity and helps manage blood glucose levels, reducing type 2 diabetes risk by 25-40%
- Weight management: Helps maintain a healthy weight and reduces risk of obesity
- Mental health: Reduces symptoms of anxiety and depression, improves mood and cognitive function
- Cancer risk: Reduces risk of several cancers including bladder, breast, colon, endometrium, esophagus, kidney, lung, and stomach cancers
- Bone health: Helps build and maintain strong bones and reduces risk of osteoporosis
- Sleep: Improves sleep quality and reduces risk of sleep disorders
- Longevity: Regular exercisers live an average of 3-7 years longer than inactive individuals

## Exercise for Specific Conditions

### Hypertension
Engage in at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity. Include dynamic resistance exercises 2-3 days per week. Avoid heavy isometric exercises (like heavy lifting with holding) if blood pressure is uncontrolled. Aim for consistency — regular shorter sessions are better than occasional long sessions.

### Type 2 Diabetes
At least 150 minutes per week of moderate-to-vigorous aerobic activity. Resistance training at least 2-3 days per week on non-consecutive days. Reduce time spent in prolonged sitting with light activity breaks every 30 minutes. Monitor blood glucose before, during, and after exercise if taking insulin or insulin secretagogues.

### Obesity
Aim for 225-420 minutes per week of moderate-intensity physical activity for weight loss. Combine aerobic activity with resistance training to preserve lean body mass. Start gradually and increase duration and intensity over time. Focus on activities that are enjoyable and sustainable for long-term adherence.

### Arthritis
Low-impact activities such as walking, cycling, swimming, or water aerobics are recommended. Start slowly and progress gradually to avoid flare-ups. Include flexibility and range-of-motion exercises daily. Strength training helps support and protect affected joints.

## Safety Considerations

Start slowly and gradually increase intensity, especially if currently inactive. Stay hydrated before, during, and after exercise. Use appropriate gear and equipment. Stop exercising and seek medical attention if experiencing chest pain, extreme shortness of breath, dizziness, or severe pain. Consult a healthcare provider before starting a new exercise program if you have a chronic health condition, are pregnant, or have recently had a cardiac event.
